01 / Senior Thesis / Princeton ORFE / April 2026
Swing Time in Division I Baseball
Bridging Bat Speed and Swing Length to Predict Hitter Success
At the collegiate level, bat speed and swing length are not tracked. This thesis introduced "Swing Time," defined as the duration from swing initiation to contact, as a proxy metric measurable through frame-by-frame Synergy video analysis synchronized with TrackMan pitch data. The core insight: a hitter who takes less time to swing has more time to read the pitch. Using a dataset of 28 Division I hitters across multiple conferences, the thesis validated Swing Time as a statistically independent, player-specific mechanical signature and demonstrated its predictive power for season-level hitting statistics.
Swing Time is statistically independent of game context, pitch type, and hitter archetype (R² < 0.05 across all tested variables), confirming it as a stable individual characteristic.
PCA of MLB Statcast bat-tracking data discovered a 1:1 standardized ratio of bat speed to swing length, with PC2 (42.7% of variance) identified as the primary Swing Time dimension.
A single-hidden-layer neural network outperformed ridge regression in predicting AVG, OBP, SLG, OPS, and JOPS in 9 of 10 random seeds, suggesting nonlinear relationships between swing timing and outcomes.
Bootstrapped sample size optimization determined that 20 swings per hitter achieves ≥90% distributional accuracy, making the methodology scalable for player evaluation pipelines.

02 / Term Paper / Princeton Psychology & CS / 2025
What Makes a Baseball Player Swing?
A Deadline-Constrained Drift Diffusion Model of MLB Hitter Decision-Making
Hitters have between 125 and 225 milliseconds to decide whether to swing. This paper modeled that binary decision across nearly 50,000 four-seam fastballs from the 2023 to 2025 MLB seasons using a deadline-constrained Drift Diffusion Model, decomposing the relative influence of visual and contextual inputs on swing probability.
Pitch location outside the strike zone (β = −7.50) is the dominant driver of take decisions, far outweighing all other inputs.
Full count (3–2) produced the highest swing likelihood across all count states; swing probability increases monotonically with most at-bat progressions.
Bases loaded is the only runner state that suppresses swing probability, suggesting hitters inadequately adjust for walk value, revealing a quantifiable, correctable bias.
